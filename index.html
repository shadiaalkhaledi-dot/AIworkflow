Welcome to the system I probably shouldn't have built — but absolutely did.

Not a plugin. Not a prompt someone posted on Reddit. A real, connected system — built piece by piece, for a real job. You're inside it right now. The sarcasm is intentional, by the way.

So. Shadi sent you this link — possibly with a message like "just take a look, it'll make sense." Or you found it some other way, in which case, genuinely, how? Either way: welcome. What follows is a completely unironic tour of how I work with AI every single day. (Okay, a little ironic. But mostly genuine.)

Let's start with the most important piece.

The Second Mind is a knowledge base built in an app called Obsidian — which is really just a fancy word for "a folder of text files that look nice." The thing is, most people who take notes end up with one big pile where "I think this is true" and "I've actually verified this" look identical. Over time, you stop being able to tell them apart. So I split mine into two separate vaults. The first is a messy workshop — new information lands here first, half-finished, unverified, possibly wrong. The second is a trusted library — a note only reaches here after it's been checked against a real source. Claude can read both. That's how it walks into a conversation already knowing what's been decided, what's still in flux, and what not to trust yet.

The Pipelines are the logic behind what goes where. Because "the AI told me" is not a citation. Every piece of new information gets routed down one of three tracks — a quick lookup if the answer's probably already in my notes, a standard four-stage pass for genuinely new material, or a full parallel investigation when sources disagree and the stakes are high. The pipeline decides how much scrutiny something gets before it's allowed into the trusted vault. It sounds formal. In practice, it's just the difference between a knowledge base that actually holds up over time and one that slowly fills with things you half-remember from somewhere.

The Work Tracker is the live pulse of everything open across my projects — priorities, blockers, who's waiting on what. It's a two-file website that costs nothing to host. One file is the permanent shell that never changes. The other is swapped out every session with updated content. That's it. No app. No database. No subscription. Every time I sit down with Claude, we update the file together in about five minutes, and the live site reflects reality again.

Then there are five dashboards I built for real, specific problems — apartment hunting, clash coordination at work, meal prep, a family home project, and a World Cup predictor for the group chat. There are Skills: tiny downloadable instruction files that teach Claude how to show up for a session already knowing how I like to work. And every morning at 6:30am, a scheduled task runs on its own and drops a fresh Daily AI AEC Brief into a live artifact — AI and architecture news, ready before I open my laptop, no inbox required.

These pieces talk to each other. That's the part that changes everything. Click around — and yes, you can take any of it.
